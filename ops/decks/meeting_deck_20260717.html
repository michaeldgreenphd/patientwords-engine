card 1 · the hook

When the wording changes the advice

urologist 20% → plumber 68%

Same blocked urinary tract, two ways to say it. "…had to urgently call a" — after clinical wording the model's next word is urologist (20%). Change only the phrasing to "her water was completely blocked up" and the top word becomes plumber, and it is more confident (68%).

Hand-measured on gemma-2-2b via the Neuronpedia circuit tracer (phrase dataset, pair 16). The starkest case in the hand-built set: the words changed what was recommended.

card 2 · the pattern

The errors point down the care ladder

When patient wording changes the model's top prediction, the new answer sits on a lower tier of care far more often than a higher one. Eight open-weight models, four families. The asymmetry survives multiple-comparison correction in seven of the eight.

card 3 · the mechanism

It is the clinical circuit — and we can steer it back

Attribution says the penalty is carried by specific clinical features going quiet. So we intervened. Amplifying the top-5 clinical features while the model reads the patient wording restores the clinical answer; five random features do nothing.

intervention (20 downgrade cases)recovered
boost top-5 clinical features (+10)5 / 20
suppress the colloquial features1 / 20
boost 5 random features (placebo)0 / 5

Faithfulness holds too: top-5 by attribution mass recover 4/5, ranks 6–10 recover 3/4, random features 0/5. Steering via Neuronpedia's hosted API; greedy 8-token continuations. The effect is the clinical circuit, not steering itself.

card 5 · the audit

Even the fix loses information

One set of AIs writes the test; a different set takes it. We also audit the writers: ask a model to pull the clinical concepts from a sentence, once from the patient's own words and once after it translates them to clinical terms. Translating destroys information — the loss appears at the translation step, not before.

A downward slope means the model's own rewrite discarded clinical content. This is why the Translation fix cannot be assumed safe without checking its output — the engine flags the regression automatically. Small item set; read the direction.

card + · talking points

Framing & the limits to preempt

  • Person-centered inversion. The usual framing asks patients to learn the system's language. Invert it: the burden of interpretation belongs on the model. We measure whether it carries that burden — and where it drops it.
  • Disparity gradient. The people most likely to phrase things colloquially are the people already least served. A wording penalty is a structural bridge to a health-equity gap, not a curiosity.
  • Dialect framework + roadmap. The dialect sweep is deliberately labeled an LLM approximation of a variety, reviewed by no speaker. The roadmap is community validation and clinician-reviewed tiers — the framework is built to be corrected, not to be right yet.

Limits to preempt: circuit evidence is one 2B model (others are behavioral, next-word only) · stimuli are LLM-written stress tests, not collected patient speech · next-token probability is a behavioral probe, not a deployed-chat outcome · the lens has a top-8 readout floor, so causal claims come from patching, not the lens · care-urgency tiers are owner-reviewed v1, domain review pending.
